Pump-probe spectroscopy can be applied to characterize the structure and dynamics of the excited states of molecules or nanomaterials. In a transient absorption experiment, a laser pulse is used to excite, or pump, the molecule or material into its excited state. The excited state then exhibits relaxation dynamics on the femtosecond or picosecond timescales. Before the system is permitted to relax fully back to its ground state, however, it is hit with a second laser pulse. This probe pulse is used to probe the population in the excited state at different temporal delays with respect to the initial excitation.

In a pump-probe spectroscopy experiment, you measure the decay in signal intensity ($I$ in arbitrary units) versus the time between the pump and probe pulses ($t$ or pump-probe delay in femtoseconds or fs). This time-resolved signal intensity is the convolution of the instrument reponse modeled by a Gaussian function with a full width half maximum of $\sigma$

$$ R(t) = A_{1} \exp \left( -\frac{t^{2}}{2\sigma^2} \right) $$

and the exponential decay of the excited states with a decay constant $\tau$

$$ S(t) = A_{2} \exp \left( -\frac{t}{\tau} \right) $$

yielding a model expression of

$$ I(t) = \int R(t-t')S(t')dt' = A \exp \left( \frac{\sigma^2}{2\tau^2} - \frac{t}{\tau} \right) \left( 1 - \text{erf} \left( \frac{\sigma^2-t\tau}{\sqrt{2}\sigma\tau} \right) \right) $$

Your system may also have a background signal with an intensity that drifts linearly with time (i.e., $I_{background}(t)=I_0+I_1t$ ) such that your measured pump-probe signal can be modeled as:

$$ I_{measured}(t) = I_{background} + I = I_0 + I_1t + A \exp \left( \frac{\sigma^2}{2\tau^2} - \frac{t}{\tau} \right) \left( 1 - \text{erf} \left( \frac{\sigma^2-t\tau}{\sqrt{2}\sigma\tau} \right) \right) $$

from scipy import stats
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit

def linear_regression(xs, ys):
    n = len(xs)
    sum_x = 0
    sum_y = 0
    sum_xy = 0
    sum_xx = 0
    for i in range(0,len(xs)):
        sum_x = sum_x + xs[i]
        sum_y = sum_y + ys[i]
        sum_xx = sum_xx + xs[i]**2
        sum_xy = sum_xy + ys[i]*xs[i]
    a_one = (n*sum_xy - sum_x*sum_y)/(n*sum_xx - sum_x**2)
    a_o = (sum_y/n) - a_one*(sum_x/n)
    return [a_one, a_o]
